RIHA: Report-Image Hierarchical Alignment for Radiology Report Generation

Yucheng Chen1 Yang Yu2 Yufei Shi1 Conghao Xiong3 Xulei Yang2 Si Yong Yeo1(✉)
1MedVisAI Lab, Lee Kong Chian School of Medicine, Nanyang Technological University (NTU), Singapore; Centre of AI in Medicine, Singapore    2Department of Machine Intellection, Institute for Infocomm Research (I²R), A*STAR, Singapore    3Department of Computer Science and Engineering, The Chinese University of Hong Kong, Hong Kong SAR, China
📄 Paper

Abstract

Radiology report generation (RRG) aims to automatically generate clinically accurate reports from medical images, but remains challenging due to the need for fine-grained alignment between complex visual features and long-form, structured textual descriptions. Existing approaches often treat reports as flat sequences, ignoring their inherent hierarchical structure and limiting cross-modal alignment.

We propose RIHA (Report-Image Hierarchical Alignment Transformer), a novel end-to-end framework that performs multi-level alignment between radiological images and reports at paragraph, sentence, and word levels. This hierarchical alignment enables more precise cross-modal correspondence, reflecting the multi-stage reasoning process in clinical practice.

Specifically, RIHA introduces a Visual Feature Pyramid (VFP) and a Text Feature Pyramid (TFP) to extract multi-scale visual features and multi-granularity textual representations. These features are aligned through a Cross-modal Hierarchical Alignment (CHA) module using optimal transport, enabling distribution-level alignment across modalities. Additionally, a Relative Positional Encoding (RPE) mechanism is integrated into the decoder to enhance token-level alignment.

Experiments on IU-Xray and MIMIC-CXR demonstrate that RIHA achieves superior performance across both natural language generation and clinical efficacy metrics.

Key Contributions

  • We propose RIHA, a hierarchical cross-modal alignment framework for radiology report generation, aligning visual and textual features at paragraph, sentence, and word levels.
  • We design a Visual Feature Pyramid (VFP) and a Text Feature Pyramid (TFP) to extract multi-scale visual features and multi-granularity textual representations.
  • We introduce a Cross-modal Hierarchical Alignment (CHA) module based on optimal transport, enabling distribution-level alignment across modalities and semantic levels.
  • We incorporate Relative Positional Encoding (RPE) into the decoder to improve token-level alignment and enhance semantic consistency in generated reports.

Motivation

Radiology reports are inherently hierarchical: paragraph-level impressions provide overall clinical context, sentence-level descriptions detail specific anatomical findings, and word-level terminology captures precise medical measurements. Existing RRG methods typically treat reports as flat token sequences, missing the opportunity to leverage this structured hierarchy. RIHA is motivated by the need to establish synchronized cross-modal correspondences at multiple semantic levels simultaneously, mirroring the hierarchical reasoning process of clinical radiologists.

Figure 1. A chest X-ray image and its corresponding radiology report, illustrating the hierarchical structure of paragraph-, sentence-, and word-level information. This highlights the need for multi-level visual-textual alignment.

Method Overview

RIHA is designed to explicitly model the hierarchical structure of radiology reports and align it with visual representations at multiple levels.

In the first stage, a Visual Feature Pyramid (VFP) extracts multi-scale visual features, while a Text Feature Pyramid (TFP) decomposes radiology reports into paragraph-, sentence-, and word-level representations. This design captures the hierarchical nature of both modalities.

In the second stage, a Cross-modal Hierarchical Alignment (CHA) module aligns visual and textual features at each semantic level. This alignment is formulated as an optimal transport problem, allowing the model to establish fine-grained correspondences between visual regions and textual elements across different granularities.

In the third stage, the aligned features are used by a transformer-based encoder-decoder to generate reports. Relative Positional Encoding (RPE) is incorporated to enhance token-level alignment by modeling contextual relationships between tokens.

This hierarchical alignment framework bridges the gap between visual features and structured clinical narratives, enabling more accurate and coherent report generation.

Figure 2. Overview of the RIHA framework. Multi-scale visual features and multi-granularity textual representations are extracted by VFP and TFP, respectively, and aligned through the CHA module before report generation.

Results

We evaluate RIHA on two benchmark datasets, IU-Xray and MIMIC-CXR, using both natural language generation (BLEU, METEOR, ROUGE-L, CIDEr) and clinical efficacy metrics.

The results show that RIHA consistently outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods across both datasets. The improvements are attributed to the proposed hierarchical alignment mechanism, which enables more precise mapping between visual features and structured report components.

Figure 3 presents qualitative comparisons of generated reports on the MIMIC-CXR dataset. Compared with the baseline model, RIHA produces reports that more accurately reflect the findings in the ground truth, as evidenced by the higher overlap of highlighted clinical terms. Notably, RIHA demonstrates improved consistency in describing key observations, such as anatomical locations and pathological conditions, reducing both omission and hallucination errors. This indicates that hierarchical alignment enables more reliable mapping between visual features and structured report components.

Figure 3. Examples of generated reports from the MIMIC-CXR testing subset using the baseline model and our proposed RIHA method. Identical findings in the ground truth (GT) and generated reports are highlighted with matching colors, demonstrating the superior performance of our approach.

Figure 4 provides further insight into how this improvement is achieved. The attention visualizations show that RIHA focuses more precisely on clinically relevant regions corresponding to each keyword, whereas the baseline model exhibits more diffuse or misaligned attention patterns. This improved localization suggests that hierarchical alignment enhances the model's ability to associate specific visual evidence with corresponding textual elements.

Figure 4. Relative positional encoding in the transformer. Compared to absolute positional encoding, RPE captures contextual relationships between tokens, improving token-level alignment.

Together, these results demonstrate that RIHA not only improves linguistic quality but also strengthens clinically meaningful visual-text alignment, which is essential for generating accurate and trustworthy radiology reports.
