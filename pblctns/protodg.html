Prototypical Representation Learning for Multi-Site Domain Generalization in Schizophrenia Diagnosis

Yixin Ji1 Vince D. Calhoun2 Jin Zhang1 Qi Zhu1 Shengrong Li1 Daniel H. Mathalon3 Si Yong Yeo4 Daoqiang Zhang1 Shile Qi1(✉)
1Nanjing University of Aeronautics and Astronautics, Nanjing, China    2Tri-institutional Center for Translational Research in Neuroimaging and Data Science, Atlanta, GA, USA    3University of California San Francisco, San Francisco, CA, USA    4MedVisAI Lab, Lee Kong Chian School of Medicine, Nanyang Technological University, Singapore
📄 IEEE TBME Paper 🔬 PubMed 📑 PDF

Abstract

Schizophrenia diagnosis from resting-state fMRI data is challenged by substantial site-induced distribution shifts in multi-site studies, where scanner differences, imaging protocols, and demographic variations introduce spurious domain-specific features that hinder model generalization.

We propose a prototypical domain generalization (ProtoDG) framework that learns site-invariant, class-discriminative representations from brain functional networks (BFNs) without accessing target domain data during training. A transformer encoder captures global inter-regional dependencies to produce rich subject-level embeddings, which are then projected into a regularized feature space using HSIC-based site-independence constraints.

A prototype learning module maps projected features to class-specific prototypes via the Sinkhorn-Knopp algorithm, with prototypes refined iteratively through exponential moving average (EMA) updates. Maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) loss optimizes feature-to-prototype matching in a probabilistic space, while contrastive and alignment losses further promote inter-class separation and intra-class compactness. The model is evaluated directly on unseen target sites without any fine-tuning.

Experiments on two independent datasets — FBIRN and BSNIP — demonstrate that our method achieves classification accuracies of 88.89%±2.22% and 86.05%±1.64%, respectively, surpassing state-of-the-art domain generalization, domain adaptation, and multi-site approaches.

Key Contributions

  • Prototypical Domain Generalization: A unified framework that combines prototype learning with domain generalization for multi-site fMRI schizophrenia classification, enabling direct evaluation on unseen target sites.
  • Sinkhorn-Knopp Prototype Assignment: Soft feature-to-prototype assignments via the Sinkhorn-Knopp algorithm, refined with EMA updates and MLE loss for stable and probabilistically principled prototype learning.
  • HSIC Site-Independence Regularization: Projected feature representations are constrained by Hilbert-Schmidt Independence Criterion (HSIC) to disentangle site-specific factors and learn site-invariant embeddings.
  • Multi-Objective Loss Design: Contrastive and alignment losses are jointly optimized to maximize inter-class separation and intra-class compactness, complementing the prototype matching objective.
  • Discriminative Brain Region Identification: The model highlights neurologically meaningful brain regions that are consistently discriminative across multiple sites, providing interpretable insights for schizophrenia biomarker discovery.

Method Overview

The proposed framework operates on brain functional networks (BFNs) constructed from resting-state fMRI recordings collected across multiple scanner sites. The pipeline consists of four main stages: feature extraction, site-invariant projection, prototype-based classification, and direct evaluation on unseen target domains.

Figure 1. Flowchart of the proposed domain generalization framework. (a) BFNs were constructed from resting-state fMRI across multiple sites and used as model input. (b) A transformer encoder extracted subject-level embeddings by modeling global inter-regional dependencies. (c) Extracted features were projected with HSIC regularization to encourage site-invariant representations; a prototype learning module assigned projected features to class-specific prototypes via the Sinkhorn-Knopp algorithm, with soft assignments guiding prototype updates through EMA; MLE loss optimized feature-to-prototype matching probabilities, while contrastive and alignment losses promoted inter-class separation and intra-class compactness. (d) The model was directly evaluated on unseen target domains for classification.

The transformer encoder captures long-range functional connectivity patterns across brain regions, producing expressive subject-level representations. These are projected into a regularized feature space where HSIC-based constraints suppress site-specific information. The prototype learning module then performs soft assignment of projected features to learnable class prototypes using the Sinkhorn-Knopp algorithm — an optimal transport technique that produces balanced, stable assignments. Prototypes are updated continuously through EMA, and the MLE loss encourages tight clustering of features around their matched prototypes in a probabilistic manner. Contrastive loss pushes features of different classes apart, while alignment loss pulls same-class features together, jointly enhancing class separability across site boundaries.

Results & Performance

The framework was evaluated in a leave-one-site-out cross-validation protocol, where the model is trained on all but one site and tested on the held-out site. Comparisons were conducted against state-of-the-art domain generalization (DG), domain adaptation (DA), and multi-site methods.

Figure 2. Classification performance in comparison with different DA methods on two datasets. (a) FBIRN, (b) BSNIP. The proposed method consistently achieves the highest accuracy across both datasets, outperforming all domain adaptation and domain generalization baselines.

Extensive ablation experiments were conducted to analyze the sensitivity of the model to key hyperparameters, including the number of prototypes, the EMA momentum coefficient, the HSIC regularization weight, and the loss balancing factors. The model demonstrates stable performance across a broad range of parameter values, confirming robustness.

Figure 3. Classification accuracies with respect to different parameter values. The model maintains competitive performance across varying hyperparameter settings, demonstrating robustness to parameter choices.

To provide neurological interpretability, the model identifies the top-10 most discriminative brain regions for schizophrenia classification. The highlighted regions are consistent with known schizophrenia-associated functional connectivity abnormalities reported in the clinical neuroscience literature.

Figure 4. Visualization of the top 10 most discriminative brain regions on two datasets. (a) FBIRN, (b) BSNIP. The identified regions are consistent with neurological findings in schizophrenia research, supporting the biological plausibility of the learned representations.
